TotalAssist Is Here, the Nation's Largest Charitable Patient Assistance Program

The Patient Advocate Foundation and PAN Foundation have merged to launch TotalAssist, the nation's largest charitable patient assistance program. Over a combined 50 years, the two organizations have helped 3.8 million patients and granted over $7 billion in financial assistance.

As of July 1, 2026, TotalAssist combines all financial assistance into one place, with nearly 150 disease-specific and health equity funds, no wait lists, and instant eligibility decisions in most cases. Grants help cover medication copays, coinsurance, deductibles, insurance premiums, office visit charges, and treatment-related costs. Eligibility generally extends to patients with income at or below 500% of the Federal Poverty Level, and a six-month lookback can reimburse recent expenses.

Apply online or call 866-512-3861 (Mon–Fri, 8:30am–5:30pm ET; press 2 for Spanish).

When Insurance Sends You Elsewhere

Some plans try to avoid paying for your drugs. Learn how to spot this and what to do.

Warning: Your Plan May Send You Away

Some plans don't want to pay for costly drugs. They may make you get free drugs from drug makers first.

What Does This Mean?

Your plan may say: "We won't pay for your drug until you ask a drug maker for free medicine first."

This saves money for your plan. But it can leave you without medicine while you wait. And you might not get the free drugs.

Three Ways Plans Do This

These tricks have fancy names. But they all do the same thing: make someone else pay for your drugs.

1

Copay Accumulator

Drug makers give you copay help. Your plan takes that help. But it does not count toward what you owe for the year.

Why This Hurts:

  • • Your copay card runs out faster
  • • When it runs out, you owe the full price
  • • You keep paying all year long
2

Copay Maximizer

Your plan spreads your copay card over the year. But the plan keeps the savings, not you.

Why This Hurts:

  • • The drug maker's help goes to your plan, not you
  • • You still pay more than you should
  • • When the help runs out, you owe a lot
3

Alternative Funding (AFP)

Your work plan says: "We won't cover this drug. Go get it free from the drug maker."

Why This Hurts:

  • • You wait weeks or months for your drugs
  • • You might not get the free drugs
  • • If you don't get free drugs, you pay full price
  • • Your plan saves money. You take the risk.

How to Tell If This Is Happening to You

Warning Signs:

  • Your plan papers say "copay accumulator"
  • Copay card money is not showing on your plan tracker
  • They tell you to call another company first
  • Your drug is "carved out" (not covered)
  • You must ask for free drugs before your plan will help

Ask Your Plan These Questions:

  • "Do you use a copay accumulator?"
  • "Will my copay card count toward what I owe?"
  • "Is my drug fully covered?"
  • "Do I have to ask for free drugs first?"

How to Protect Yourself

1
Read Your Plan Papers

Look for words like "accumulator," "maximizer," or "alternative funding." These are clues.

2
Keep Track of What You Pay

Save your receipts. Check if your copay card money shows up on your plan's tracker. If not, you may have an accumulator plan.

3
Appeal If They Say No

If your plan won't cover your drug, you can fight back. Ask your transplant team to write a letter saying you need this drug.

4
Call Your State for Help

Many states have laws to protect you. Call your state insurance office. They can tell you your rights and help you file a complaint.

5
Talk to Your Boss (If You Get Insurance From Work)

Your boss picks the plan. Tell HR how this hurts you. Ask them to pick a better plan.

States That Protect You

Some states made laws to stop these tricks. In these states, copay card money must count toward what you owe.

States With Laws to Help (as of 2026):

Arizona, Arkansas, California, Connecticut, Delaware, Georgia, Illinois, Kentucky, Louisiana, Maine, Maryland, Minnesota, Missouri, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, Puerto Rico, Tennessee, Texas, Virginia, Washington, West Virginia, and more.

Call your state insurance office to learn about new laws. More states add protections every year.

Why Transplant Patients Must Know This

  • 1

    You can't skip your drugs. Missing doses can hurt your transplant. Waiting weeks for free drugs is risky.

  • 2

    Free drugs are not a sure thing. Drug makers have rules. You might not qualify. The money might run out.

  • 3

    You pay for your plan. You pay every month. But some plans make drug makers pay for your drugs instead.

  • 4

    Big bills can hit fast. When your copay card runs out, you may owe a lot of money all at once.

Need Help?

Think this is happening to you? Talk to your transplant center's money helper or social worker. They know how to help.

You can also call the Patient Advocate Foundation for free help.
