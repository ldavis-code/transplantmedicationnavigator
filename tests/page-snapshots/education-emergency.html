TotalAssist Is Here, the Nation's Largest Charitable Patient Assistance Program

The Patient Advocate Foundation and PAN Foundation have merged to launch TotalAssist, the nation's largest charitable patient assistance program. Over a combined 50 years, the two organizations have helped 3.8 million patients and granted over $7 billion in financial assistance.

As of July 1, 2026, TotalAssist combines all financial assistance into one place, with nearly 150 disease-specific and health equity funds, no wait lists, and instant eligibility decisions in most cases. Grants help cover medication copays, coinsurance, deductibles, insurance premiums, office visit charges, and treatment-related costs. Eligibility generally extends to patients with income at or below 500% of the Federal Poverty Level, and a six-month lookback can reimburse recent expenses.

Apply online or call 866-512-3861 (Mon–Fri, 8:30am–5:30pm ET; press 2 for Spanish).

Out of medication, or about to be?

Anti-rejection medicine is not something you can safely skip. If cost or a refill problem means you are running low, act today. Here is how to get a bridge supply while you sort out the cost.

If this is a medical emergency, call 911.

Signs of rejection, fever, pain over the transplant, less urine, sudden weight gain, or feeling very unwell, need your transplant team right away. Do not wait. Never change or stop your doses on your own.

1

Call your transplant team first, today

This is the most important step. Your transplant center handles this all the time. Ask for the transplant pharmacist or social worker. They can often send an emergency prescription, give you samples, or start a fast assistance request the same day. Tell them plainly: "I am about to run out and I cannot afford the refill."

2

Ask your pharmacy for an emergency supply

Many states let a pharmacist give a few days of a regular medicine in an emergency so you do not miss doses. Ask: "Can you do an emergency fill while I sort out the cost or the refill?" Bring your pill bottle or the medicine name and dose.

3

Ask the drug maker about a bridge supply

Some manufacturers give a free short-term "bridge" or "quick start" supply while your Patient Assistance Program application is reviewed, so you are covered during the wait. Your transplant team can request this, or call the drug maker's patient support line.

4

Check foundations for urgent help

Some copay foundations review urgent cases faster. It is worth a call when you are in a pinch. See the trusted groups in our directory.

Have this ready when you call

It makes every call faster:

  • ✓ Your medicine names and doses
  • ✓ Your pharmacy's name and phone
  • ✓ Your insurance card (if you have one)
  • ✓ Your transplant center's phone number

Do not just stop taking your medicine.

Missing anti-rejection doses can put your transplant at risk. If you truly cannot get a full supply, ask your transplant team whether a safe short-term plan is possible, but let them make that call, not you.
