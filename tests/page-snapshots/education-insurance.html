TotalAssist Is Here, the Nation's Largest Charitable Patient Assistance Program

The Patient Advocate Foundation and PAN Foundation have merged to launch TotalAssist, the nation's largest charitable patient assistance program. Over a combined 50 years, the two organizations have helped 3.8 million patients and granted over $7 billion in financial assistance.

As of July 1, 2026, TotalAssist combines all financial assistance into one place, with nearly 150 disease-specific and health equity funds, no wait lists, and instant eligibility decisions in most cases. Grants help cover medication copays, coinsurance, deductibles, insurance premiums, office visit charges, and treatment-related costs. Eligibility generally extends to patients with income at or below 500% of the Federal Poverty Level, and a six-month lookback can reimburse recent expenses.

Apply online or call 866-512-3861 (Mon–Fri, 8:30am–5:30pm ET; press 2 for Spanish).

Coverage 101

How you pay for transplant medications.

The four most common ways patients are covered, what to know, what to watch for, what to do next.

Who qualifies

  • Employees of companies that offer benefits
  • Self-purchased plans through Healthcare.gov
  • Dependents on a family member's plan

How it works

You pay a monthly premium. Insurance starts paying more after you hit your deductible. Copays and coinsurance apply at the pharmacy until you reach your out-of-pocket max.

⚠ Watch for

High out-of-pocket before you hit your deductible. Narrow networks. Prior-authorization delays.

→ Next steps

  1. Check your plan's formulary for transplant medications.
  2. Know your deductible and out-of-pocket max.
  3. Confirm your transplant center is in-network.

Who qualifies

  • Adults 65+
  • People with qualifying disabilities (24+ months on SSDI)
  • Anyone with End-Stage Renal Disease

How it works

Part A covers hospital stays. Part B covers outpatient care, and your transplant anti-rejection drugs when Medicare helped pay for the transplant. Part D covers most other prescription drugs. Many enroll in Medicare Advantage for bundled coverage.

⚠ Watch for

Premiums for Part B and Part D add up. Copay cards are not allowed with Medicare.

→ Next steps

  1. Enroll in Medicare Part A, B, and D (or Advantage).
  2. Apply for Extra Help if your income is limited.
  3. Review your Part D drug formulary every fall.
  4. Kidney transplant? Ask Social Security about Part B-ID before your Medicare ends.

Who qualifies

  • Households below your state's income limit
  • Children, pregnant patients, and people with disabilities
  • Some long-term care and waiver recipients

How it works

The state pays for most covered services with low or zero out-of-pocket cost. Each state runs its own program, your benefits, prescription rules, and provider list depend on where you live.

⚠ Watch for

Income and asset limits apply. Exceed them and you can lose coverage. Provider networks may be limited. Copay cards are not allowed.

→ Next steps

  1. Contact your state Medicaid office to apply.
  2. Gather income, residency, and household documents.
  3. Re-verify eligibility on the renewal date.

Who qualifies

  • Hospital charity / financial-assistance programs
  • Manufacturer Patient Assistance Programs (PAPs)
  • Discount cards and pharmacy savings programs

How it works

You receive the full retail bill. Cash prices for transplant medications can run hundreds to thousands per month. Hospitals and pharmacies often negotiate or offer payment plans on request.

⚠ Watch for

Catastrophic out-of-pocket costs. Risk of medical debt. Skipped doses if cost feels impossible.

→ Next steps

  1. Apply for manufacturer Patient Assistance Programs first.
  2. Negotiate cash prices and ask about payment plans.
  3. Re-check Medicaid and Marketplace plan eligibility.

Educational information only. Eligibility rules and coverage details change, verify with your insurer, state Medicaid office, and transplant team.

New in 2026: CMS WISeR Prior Authorization Pilot

Starting January 2026, CMS launched the WISeR (Wasteful and Inappropriate Service Reduction) model. This adds prior authorization for 17 outpatient Medicare Part B services in 6 states.

Affected States

  • Arizona
  • New Jersey
  • Ohio
  • Oklahoma
  • Texas
  • Washington

What Transplant Patients Should Know

  • WISeR covers outpatient procedures (nerve stimulators, spinal procedures, wound care), not transplant medications
  • Your immunosuppressant drugs are NOT affected
  • If you need any of the 17 listed procedures (e.g., wound care with skin substitutes), your provider must get prior authorization
  • Coverage decisions are made within 72 hours (48 hours for urgent cases)

The WISeR model runs from January 2026 through December 2031. It applies only to Original Medicare (fee-for-service), not Medicare Advantage plans. If you are in one of these states and need a covered procedure, talk to your provider about the prior authorization process.

Each type of insurance has its own benefits, costs, and best ways to save.

This infographic compares what transplant medication help is available across four insurance types. Commercial Insurance: Insurance through your job or that you buy on your own. You may qualify for manufacturer copay cards, patient assistance programs, and copay foundations. You pay monthly premiums, copays, and deductibles, then insurance covers the rest. Common problems include high deductibles, prior authorization requirements, and copay accumulators. Next step: search your medications to find copay cards and assistance programs. Medicare: Government insurance for people 65 and older or with disabilities. You may qualify for patient assistance programs, Extra Help or Low Income Subsidy, and copay foundations. Part D covers most transplant drugs with monthly premiums and copays. Common problems include Part D costs and you cannot use manufacturer copay cards. Next step: check if you qualify for Extra Help and search for patient assistance programs. Medicaid: Government insurance for people with low income. You may qualify for patient assistance programs and state-specific programs. Covers most transplant drugs with very low or no copays. Common problems include limited pharmacy networks, formulary restrictions, and coverage ending if income changes. Next step: search your medications and check with your state Medicaid office. Uninsured or Self-Pay: No current insurance coverage. You may qualify for manufacturer patient assistance programs for free medications, state programs, and discount cards. You pay the full retail price without help. Common problems include very high costs, no negotiated rates, and difficulty affording specialty medications. Next step: apply for patient assistance programs immediately as most offer free medications for qualifying patients. The infographic concludes with an encouraging message: You are not alone and there is help available no matter what type of insurance you have.

Medicare Guide

Medicare Parts for Transplant Drugs

Part A (Hospital)
  • Induction agents
  • (Thymoglobulin, Simulect)
Part B (Medical)
  • IV infusions (Belatacept)
  • Anti-rejection drugs (if Part B-ID eligible)
  • 2026: $202.90/month premium, $283 deductible
  • Part B-ID premium: $121.60/month (kidney patients)
Part C (Medicare Advantage)
  • Combines A, B, and usually D
  • May have extra benefits
  • You must use certain doctors
Part D (Prescription)
  • Tacrolimus
  • Mycophenolate
  • Valcyte, etc.

Part D vs Medicare Advantage: Choosing Your Plan

Part D (Traditional)

  • Works with any Medicare-accepting provider
  • Separate deductible and premium
  • $2,100 out-of-pocket cap (2026)

Medicare Advantage (MA-PD)

  • Often lower or $0 premiums
  • Provider networks may be limited
  • $2,100 out-of-pocket cap (2026)

Commercial Insurance

Your main payer for the first 30 months after transplant.

Key Points

  • Can use most drug maker PAPs
  • Copay cards often work
  • Foundations help with high copays
  • May need to use a certain pharmacy

Best Strategy

Start with drug maker PAPs, then use foundations for any copays or deductibles left over.
