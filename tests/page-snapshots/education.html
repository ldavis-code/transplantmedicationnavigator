TotalAssist Is Here, the Nation's Largest Charitable Patient Assistance Program

The Patient Advocate Foundation and PAN Foundation have merged to launch TotalAssist, the nation's largest charitable patient assistance program. Over a combined 50 years, the two organizations have helped 3.8 million patients and granted over $7 billion in financial assistance.

As of July 1, 2026, TotalAssist combines all financial assistance into one place, with nearly 150 disease-specific and health equity funds, no wait lists, and instant eligibility decisions in most cases. Grants help cover medication copays, coinsurance, deductibles, insurance premiums, office visit charges, and treatment-related costs. Eligibility generally extends to patients with income at or below 500% of the Federal Poverty Level, and a six-month lookback can reimburse recent expenses.

Apply online or call 866-512-3861 (Mon–Fri, 8:30am–5:30pm ET; press 2 for Spanish).

The Deductible Trap

Why Prescription Discount Cards Can Cost Transplant Patients More

⚠️ Important Warning for Transplant Patients

Using discount cards like GoodRx or SingleCare can cost you THOUSANDS of extra dollars each year.

Watch out for the "deductible trap." Here's what happens:

If you use a discount card, the money you pay does NOT count toward your deductible.

You still have to pay your full deductible later. This can cost you more money over the year.

What to do: Ask your insurance company when to start using discount cards.

Real-World Example

✅ The Right Way

Using Insurance

  • Month 1-3: Pay $2,100 out-of-pocket (reaching your yearly limit)
  • Month 4-12: Insurance pays 100% = $0 out-of-pocket
  • Total Annual Cost: $2,100
❌ The Discount Card Trap

Using GoodRx/SingleCare

  • Month 1-12: Pay discounted price (~$200/month) but NONE counts toward yearly limit
  • Result: You NEVER reach your yearly limit, so you pay all year long
  • Total Annual Cost: $2,400

Small savings today = thousands of dollars in extra costs over the year.

When Should Transplant Patients Use Discount Cards?

Scenario 1: Drug NOT Covered by Insurance

For a medicine NOT on your insurance's list and no cheaper generic option is available.

Scenario 2: One-Time, Low-Cost Medications

For short-term medicines not related to your transplant (like a round of antibiotics) where the discount price is less than your copay.

Scenario 3: Extremely High Deductible

If you have a very high deductible AND the cash price is much lower, but even then, think hard about the cost of not reaching your yearly limit.

🛑 NEVER Use Discount Cards For:

Your daily anti-rejection medicines like Tacrolimus, Mycophenolate, or Cyclosporine.

These are the medicines that will help you reach your yearly limit fast.

Better Alternatives

Patient Assistance Programs (PAPs)

Free or low-cost medicines straight from drug makers.

✅ Counts toward your yearly limit

Co-pay Foundations

Groups that help pay for your copays and deductibles.

✅ Counts toward your yearly limit

Drug Maker Copay Cards

Help from drug makers for brand-name medicines (not for Medicare patients).

✅ Counts toward your yearly limit

Key Takeaways

  • 1

    Your goal every year is to hit your yearly spending limit (the most you pay each year). Once you reach it, insurance pays 100% for the rest of the year.

  • 2

    Discount cards stop your payments from counting toward this goal. They may save you a few dollars per medicine, but cost you thousands over the year.

  • 3

    Always use PAPs and co-pay foundations first. These programs help you pay for medicines AND help you reach your yearly limit faster.

  • 4

    Talk to your transplant team. They can connect you with people who know these programs and can help you through the system.

Questions?

Not sure if a discount card is right for you? Ask your transplant center's money helper or social worker. They can look at your insurance plan and help you choose.
