You Can Do This!

A denial is NOT the final answer. Many people feel scared or confused when their medication is denied. That's normal. This guide will walk you through exactly what to do, step by step.

Part 1: Understanding Your Denial

Before you can fight a denial, you need to understand why it happened. Look at your denial letter carefully. Here are the most common reasons insurance companies say "no":

Prior Authorization Needed

Your doctor must get permission first before insurance will pay.

Not on the Formulary

Your drug isn't on their approved list of medications.

Step Therapy Required

They want you to try a cheaper drug first.

Quantity Limits

They say you're prescribed too much medication.

Generic Preferred

They want you to use a generic instead of brand name.

Medical Necessity

They don't think you need this specific medication.

Important for Transplant Patients

Transplant medications are often "narrow therapeutic index" drugs. This means even small changes in your blood levels can cause serious problems. Your doctor may have good reasons for prescribing a specific medication that generic alternatives cannot replace.

Part 2: Know Your Rights

You Have the Right To:

  • Get a written explanation - The insurance company must tell you exactly why they denied your medication
  • Appeal any denial - You can always ask them to look at your case again
  • Request a fast review - If waiting could harm your health, you can ask for an urgent decision
  • Get an outside review - If the insurance company says "no" again, someone from outside the company can review your case
  • File a complaint - You can report problems to your state insurance department

Part 3: How to Appeal - Step by Step

1

Get Your Denial in Writing

Call the number on your insurance card and ask for a written denial letter. This letter tells you exactly why they said no and how long you have to appeal.

What to Say:
I need a copy of the denial letter for my medication [medication name]. Please send it to me in writing and tell me the deadline to appeal.
2

Mark Your Calendar

You usually have 30 to 60 days to appeal. Write the deadline on your calendar right away. Set a reminder on your phone 2 weeks before the deadline.

3

Contact Your Transplant Team

Call your transplant coordinator or doctor's office. Tell them about the denial. Ask them to write a "letter of medical necessity" that explains why you need this specific medication.

What to Say:
My insurance denied my [medication name]. I need to appeal by [date]. Can my doctor write a letter of medical necessity explaining why I need this specific medication for my transplant?
4

Gather Your Documents

Collect everything you need for your appeal (see the checklist below). Make copies of everything - never send originals.

5

Write Your Appeal Letter

Write a short letter asking them to reverse the denial. Keep it simple and include: your name, member ID, the medication name, and why you need it.

6

Submit Your Appeal

Send everything together. Use certified mail or fax so you have proof it was received. Keep copies of everything you send.

Part 4: Your Appeal Checklist

Use this checklist to make sure you have everything you need:

Documents to Gather:

  • Copy of the denial letter
  • Letter of medical necessity from your transplant doctor
  • Recent lab results (drug levels, kidney/liver function)
  • Copy of your transplant summary
  • Your appeal letter (written by you)
  • Any other medical records that support your case

Information to Include in Your Appeal Letter:

  • Your full name and date of birth
  • Your insurance member ID number
  • The claim or reference number from the denial letter
  • Name of the medication that was denied
  • Date of the denial
  • Short explanation of why you need this medication
  • Your contact information (phone and address)
  • Your signature and the date

Before You Send:

  • Made copies of everything
  • Double-checked the deadline
  • Found the correct address to send it to
  • Chosen a method with tracking (certified mail or fax)

Part 5: Need a Fast Decision?

Request an Expedited (Fast) Appeal If:
  • You are running out of your transplant medication
  • Waiting could cause serious harm to your health
  • Your doctor says your situation is urgent

For an expedited appeal, the insurance company usually must give you an answer within 72 hours (3 days) instead of the normal 30 days.

What to Say:
I need an expedited appeal for my transplant medication. Running out of this medication is life-threatening for transplant patients. My doctor [name] can confirm this is urgent.

Part 6: If Your Appeal Is Denied

Don't give up! You have more options:

Internal Appeal (Second Level)
Many insurance plans allow a second appeal reviewed by different people at the company. Ask if this is available.
External Review
You can request a review by an independent organization outside the insurance company. This is your legal right.
State Insurance Department
File a complaint with your state's insurance commissioner. They can investigate and sometimes help resolve issues.
Patient Advocacy Organizations
Groups like the Patient Advocate Foundation (1-800-532-5274) offer free help with appeals.

Part 8: Sample Appeal Letter

Here is a simple template you can use. Fill in the blanks with your own information:

[Your Name]
[Your Address]
[City, State ZIP]
[Your Phone Number]
[Your Email]

[Date]

[Insurance Company Name]
[Appeals Department Address]
[City, State ZIP]

RE: Appeal for Coverage of [Medication Name]
Member Name: [Your Name]
Member ID: [Your ID Number]
Claim/Reference Number: [From denial letter]

Dear Appeals Committee:

I am writing to appeal the denial of coverage for [medication name and dose]. I received a denial letter dated [date] stating [brief reason for denial].

I am a [organ type] transplant recipient. I received my transplant on [date] at [hospital name]. I need this medication to prevent my body from rejecting my transplant.

My transplant doctor, [doctor's name], has written a letter of medical necessity explaining why I need this specific medication. I have included this letter along with my recent lab results and transplant records.

[If applicable: I have tried [other medication names] in the past, but they did not work well for me because [brief reason].]

Please reverse this denial and approve coverage for [medication name]. Without this medication, I am at risk of organ rejection.

Please contact me at [phone number] if you need any additional information.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,

[Your Signature]
[Your Printed Name]
[Date]

Enclosures:

  • Copy of denial letter
  • Letter of medical necessity from Dr. [Name]
  • Recent lab results
  • Transplant summary

Part 9: Tips for a Successful Appeal

DO:
  • Stay calm and be polite
  • Keep records of all phone calls (date, time, name of person you spoke with)
  • Get everything in writing
  • Follow up if you don't hear back
  • Meet all deadlines
  • Ask your doctor's office for help
DON'T:
  • Don't give up after the first "no"
  • Don't miss the deadline
  • Don't send original documents (only copies)
  • Don't stop taking your medication without talking to your doctor
  • Don't feel embarrassed to ask for help

Part 10: While You Wait for a Decision

Don't Run Out of Medication!

Ask your doctor or pharmacist about these options while your appeal is being reviewed:

  • Emergency supply: Some pharmacies can give you a few days' worth of medication
  • Samples: Your doctor may have free samples
  • Patient assistance programs: Drug companies often help patients who can't afford their medications
  • Manufacturer coupons: Some brands offer discount cards

Remember: You Are Not Alone

Thousands of transplant patients successfully appeal their denials every year. You deserve access to the medications that keep you healthy. Don't give up!
