New · Now Live

TotalAssist Is Here, the Nation's Largest Charitable Patient Assistance Program

The Patient Advocate Foundation and PAN Foundation have merged to launch TotalAssist, the nation's largest charitable patient assistance program. Over a combined 50 years, the two organizations have helped 3.8 million patients and granted over $7 billion in financial assistance.

As of July 1, 2026, TotalAssist combines all financial assistance into one place, with nearly 150 disease-specific and health equity funds, no wait lists, and instant eligibility decisions in most cases. Grants help cover medication copays, coinsurance, deductibles, insurance premiums, office visit charges, and treatment-related costs. Eligibility generally extends to patients with income at or below 500% of the Federal Poverty Level, and a six-month lookback can reimburse recent expenses.

Apply online or call 866-512-3861 (Mon–Fri, 8:30am–5:30pm ET; press 2 for Spanish).

Mixing Programs

Most transplant patients use a mix of help programs to pay for their medicines. It's like a puzzle.

Effective January 2026

Medicare Drug Price Negotiation

For the first time ever, Medicare talked drug makers into lower prices.

The first new prices start January 1, 2026, with big savings. Examples: Eliquis drops from $521 to $231/month (56% less), and Januvia drops from $527 to $113/month (79% less). This will save Medicare patients $1.5 billion in 2026 alone.

The first 10 drugs include medicines for heart failure (Entresto), diabetes (Jardiance, Farxiga, Januvia), blood clots (Eliquis, Xarelto), immune diseases (Stelara, Enbrel), blood cancers (Imbruvica), and insulin (NovoLog/Fiasp).

About 8.8 million Medicare Part D patients used these medicines in 2023. A second round of 15 drugs was selected for 2027 prices.

Third Round Announced January 27, 2026

CMS selected 15 new drugs for negotiation, including the first-ever Part B drugs. New prices take effect January 1, 2028.

Transplant-relevant picks: Orencia (abatacept, related to Belatacept), Trulicity (dulaglutide, for post-transplant diabetes), Xolair (omalizumab, lung transplant), Anoro Ellipta (lung transplant). Also includes Botox, Biktarvy, Cimzia, Cosentyx, Entyvio, Erleada, Kisqali, Lenvima, Rexulti, Verzenio, and Xeljanz.

New in Mid-2026

Medicare GLP-1 Coverage for Weight Loss

A big change: Medicare will now cover weight loss drugs.

Starting mid-2026, Medicare will cover certain GLP-1 drugs for weight loss, ending a rule that kept these drugs out of Part D.

Covered drugs: Wegovy and Zepbound will be covered for patients with weight-related health issues. Your copay should be about $50/month after you meet your deductible.

Eli Lilly and Novo Nordisk agreed to give these drugs at lower prices for Medicare patients.

Medicare Prescription Payment Plan

A big change from new laws

Since 2025, Medicare Part D patients can spread out their drug costs over the year instead of paying large amounts all at once.

This program makes your costs more steady and easier to manage.

Ask your Part D plan about this payment plan during open enrollment.

Part D vs Medicare Advantage: Picking Your Plan

Knowing the differences can save you a lot of money on transplant medicines.

Medicare Part D (Traditional)

A drug plan that works with Original Medicare. It's a separate plan just for medicines.

  • ✓ Works with any Medicare-accepting provider
  • ✓ Separate deductible and premium
  • ✓ $2,100 out-of-pocket cap (2026, up from $2,000 in 2025)

Medicare Advantage (MA-PD)

An all-in-one plan that covers both doctor visits AND medicines.

  • ✓ Often lower or $0 premiums
  • ✓ Provider networks may be limited
  • ✓ $2,100 out-of-pocket cap (2026, up from $2,000 in 2025)

Medicare Parts for Transplant Drugs

Knowing which part of Medicare pays for your transplant medicines is key.

Part A (Hospital)

Pays for drugs given while you're in the hospital.

  • Induction agents (Thymoglobulin, Simulect)

Part B (Medical)

Pays for some drugs you get at clinics. Also covers anti-rejection drugs if you don't have Part D.

  • IV infusions (Belatacept/Nulojix)

Part D (Prescription)

Pays for most of the pills you take at home each day.

  • Tacrolimus, Mycophenolate, etc.

Strategies by Insurance Type

  2. Use Foundations for deductibles.

Insurance Words Made Simple

Knowing what these words mean helps you handle costs and find help.

Premium

The amount you pay each month to have insurance. Think of it like a gym membership - you pay every month whether you use it or not.

The amount you must pay yourself before your insurance starts helping. If your deductible is $500, you pay the first $500 of medicine costs. After that, insurance helps pay.

(or Co-payment)

A fixed amount you pay each time you get medicine. Example: You might pay $10 for each prescription, and insurance pays the rest.

The percentage you pay after meeting your deductible. If you have 20% coinsurance, you pay $20 for every $100 of medicine cost. Insurance pays the other $80.

Yearly Spending Limit ( )

The most money you will pay in one year. Once you reach this amount, insurance pays 100% of covered medicines. This is your safety net.

Covered Medicines List ( )

The list of medicines your insurance will cover. Think of it as the insurance company's menu. If your medicine isn't on the list, you might pay more or need special approval.

Tier

How insurance groups medicines by cost. Lower tiers (1-2) cost less. Higher tiers (3-4-5) cost more.

  • Tier 1: Usually generic drugs (lowest cost)
  • Tier 2: Preferred brand drugs
  • Tier 3-5: Non-preferred or specialty drugs (highest cost)

When you need insurance company approval before they will pay for a medicine. Your doctor must explain why you need that specific medicine.

New in 2026: CMS WISeR Pilot

The WISeR (Wasteful and Inappropriate Service Reduction) model requires prior authorization for 17 outpatient Medicare Part B services in 6 states: Arizona, New Jersey, Ohio, Oklahoma, Texas, and Washington. While it does not directly affect transplant medications, it may add steps for some procedures. Learn more on our Appeals page.

Medicine

A medicine that works the same as a brand name but costs less. Like store-brand cereal versus name-brand - same thing, different package.

Brand Name Medicine

The original version of a medicine made by the company that invented it. Usually costs more than generic.

Medication

Expensive medicines that need special handling or monitoring. Most transplant medicines are specialty medications. They often cost $600+ per month.

Medicare Part D

The part of Medicare that helps pay for prescription medicines. You must sign up for this separately.

Yearly Out-of-Pocket Cap

A yearly limit on what you pay out of pocket for Medicare Part D drugs. In 2026 the cap is $2,100. Once you reach it, you pay $0 for covered drugs for the rest of the year.

Catastrophic Coverage

After you spend a lot on medicines in one year, Medicare pays almost everything. You only pay a small amount (about $4-$12) per prescription.

In-Network Pharmacy

A pharmacy that works with your insurance. You pay less here. Think of it as a preferred partner.

Out-of-Network Pharmacy

A pharmacy that doesn't have a deal with your insurance. You might pay more or get no coverage here.

PBM (Pharmacy Benefit Manager)

The company that manages prescription drug coverage for your insurance. They decide which medicines are covered and how much they cost.

Patient Assistance Program ( )

Free or low-cost medicine programs run by drug companies. If you can't afford your medicine, these programs might help.

Copay Card or Coupon

Help from drug companies to lower what you pay for brand name medicines. Important: These often don't work with Medicare.

Manufacturer Assistance

Help directly from the company that makes your medicine. This can be free medicine, copay help, or discounts.

Mail Order Pharmacy

A pharmacy that ships medicine to your home. Usually gives you 90 days of medicine at once. Often costs less than getting 30 days at a local pharmacy.

Step Therapy

When insurance requires you to try a cheaper medicine first before they'll pay for a more expensive one. Like having to try the basic version before getting the premium version.

Exclusion

A medicine that insurance will not cover at all. You must pay the full price yourself or find other help.

Why This Matters for Transplant Patients

Your anti-rejection medicines are specialty medications. They are expensive. Understanding these words helps you:

  • Know what you'll pay
  • Find help when costs are too high
  • Talk to your transplant team about options
  • Apply for assistance programs

Questions to Ask

  • What tier are my transplant medicines?
  • How much is my deductible?
  • When do I reach my yearly spending limit?
  • Are my medicines on the covered medicines list?
  • Can I use mail order to save money?
